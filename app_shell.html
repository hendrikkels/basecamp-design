The discipline of things.

A short essay about why I keep building small tools — and why I keep walking away from them before they grow up. The half-built is a place to live in, not just leave.

▰▰Every tool I build starts as a half-thought. A note in a textfile, a sketch in a notebook, a feature I wish another app had. I let it sit for a week. Sometimes a month. If it’s still bothering me, I open a new project.

Most don’t survive contact with a deadline. basecamp nearly didn’t — three weeks in I was tempted to scrap the dashboard and turn it into a static site. I’m glad I waited.

1. The boring stack

▱▱The room runs on the smallest pile of tools I could find. Plain HTML, plain CSS, a tiny JS runtime for the widgets. No build server, no Docker, no dependency I haven’t personally read the source of. vite build and rsync to a CDN. That’s it.

The boring stack is the one you ship with. It’s also the one you maintain six months later without flinching, which is the whole point.

If you can’t maintain it on a Sunday afternoon, you don’t own it.

2. Restraint, by design

░░One accent color. Two fonts. Five sizes of type and five sizes of space. I held myself to this for the whole build, and the design got better every time I bumped into a wall.

Constraints aren’t a posture. They’re a discipline that protects future you from current you.
